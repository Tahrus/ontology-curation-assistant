## ABSTRACT

This work presents a methodology for evaluating the effectiveness of hybrid modelling under varying conditions of mechanistic model quality and information available for model training, that is typically expressed as the amount of data available. While hybrid models – which integrate mechanistic and data-driven components – have gained significant attention in process systems engineering, their advantages over purely mechanistic or data-driven alternatives remain inadequately quantified. We address this gap by investigating two critical fac tors: (i) the impact of mechanistic model fidelity on hybrid model performance, and (ii) the influence of cali bration dataset size on prediction accuracy. Our methodology is validated through an in-silico case study of baker's yeast cultivation and a real-world industrial application of ion-exchange chromatography in biophar maceutical manufacturing. Results demonstrate that hybrid models consistently outperform purely mechanistic and data-driven approaches when the mechanistic component captures fundamental process behaviours, even with structural simplifications. Notably, hybrid models maintain superior predictive capability in extrapolative scenarios; however, when mechanistic knowledge is severely limited and insufficient information is available for compensation, hybridisation benefits diminish substantially. The work provides quantitative guidance for practitioners to determine when hybrid modelling represents a justified investment of modelling resources in process engineering applications.

## 1. Introduction

In recent years, a variety of modelling approaches have gained sig nificant attention to support the design and optimisation of engineering processes across diverse industrial sectors (Zhang et al., 2019). The growing interest in adopting mathematical models is motivated by the increasing demand for more efficient and reliable approaches to process development and decision-making, especially in contexts where experi mental campaigns are costly, time-consuming, or limited by operational constraints (Wang et al., 2024). The level of knowledge and understanding of the process, as well as the amount of information required for cali bration and validation of a model at a given stage of process development, can vary significantly, affecting the selection of the modelling method (Chen and Ierapetritou, 2020; Zendehboudi et al., 2018). Depending on the extent of the underlying physics that is captured by the model equations, mathematical models are generally categorized into three main types (Bonvin et al., 2016; Moser et al., 2020): mecha nistic models (also referred to as knowledge-driven, first-principles, or white-box models), data-driven models (often called empirical or black-box models), and hybrid models (also known as semi-empirical or grey box models). One of the primary benefits of mechanistic models is their ability to enhance understanding of products and processes by explicitly describing the physical, chemical, and biological properties of the system under investigation. They typically require few calibration experiments and provide superior extrapolation capabilities compared to data-driven approaches. However, deriving their equations may be complex, as it involves identifying the exact underlying physical phe nomena that characterize the process under analysis, and demands more time, resources, and modelling effort, often requiring high computa tional burden (Solle et al., 2017). On the other hand, empirical models only rely on available historical data. The main advantages include their ability to capture correlation structures within data with no need of detailed understanding of the underlying physics, rapid development timelines, and reduced compu tational demands, particularly when they offer simplified representa tions of highly complex mechanistic models (Reis and Saraiva, 2019; Rendall and Reis, 2018). Nonetheless, they typically require extensive calibration datasets, which may be limited or inconsistent in terms of structure and quality. Furthermore, generalization and interpretability can be more challenging since their performance may strongly reduce when applied to explorative scenarios (Sansana et al., 2021). When tackling real-world problems, all available sources of infor mation (i.e., physical understanding of the system and historical process data) should be leveraged. In this context, hybrid modelling strategies are effective alternatives aiming to integrate the physical knowledge embedded in the available mechanistic model with empirical data (von Stosch et al., 2014). As a result, hybrid configurations preserve core insight into the underlying physical process mechanisms while extract ing information from available data-driven patterns that cannot be captured by first-principles knowledge, thereby enhancing both pre dictive accuracy and interpretability (Sansana et al., 2021). Addition ally, hybrid models can contribute to reducing the overall time required for model development. In general, extending a mechanistic model to include more detailed knowledge can be a time-consuming task (Schweidtmann et al., 2024). By leveraging the flexibility of data-driven components, part of the knowledge not explicitly captured in the mechanistic equations can instead be extracted by appropriately modelling the available process data, thereby accelerating the con struction of reliable process models. Over the last years, various studies have explored the potential of hybrid models across different domains. For instance, Sch¨afer et al. (2019) used artificial neural networks combined with differential equations to model rectification columns, and the model was later used for real-time applications (Lüthje et al., 2021). Bui et al. Shah et al. (2022) demonstrated the high predictive capability of a deep neural network-based hybrid model for an industry-scale fermentation process, outperforming a purely mechanistic kinetic model. Polak et al. (2023) developed a hybrid modelling approach to evaluate the effects of raw material variations and process parameter fluctuations on product quantity and quality in a flow chemistry process for small molecules manufacturing. Xie et al. (2024) employed a neural network to compensate for the unmodeled dynamics of the first-principles model on an industrial nitration process under load-changing scenarios. Mahanty et al. (2025) proposed a hybrid model to predict diesel fuel removal dynamics, with the instantaneous removal rate modelled using an artificial neural network. Michalopoulou and Papathanasiou (2025) integrated artificial neural networks with process knowledge to describe the nonlinear dynamics of a chromatographic separation process. Caspari et al. (2025) provided evidence that hybrid models remain effective in data-scarce scenarios, highlighting their robustness with limited information. Steinacker et al. (2025) explored the potential of hybrid models based on different neural networks and compared the performance to typically used mechanistic models to predict atypical trajectories of hematotoxicity damage to chemotherapy patients. Wang et al. (2025) demonstrated the improved performances of physics-data-driven hybrid models to predict accurately the friction torque in hydraulic motors. However, not only do hybrid models require a careful integration of system knowledge and historical data, but they also necessitate a deep understanding of when and how each component of the model (i.e., the mechanistic and data-driven ones) can contribute to the overall hybrid performance (Rogers et al., 2023; Bradley et al., 2022). Developing a comprehensive framework to systematically construct and integrate these components remains challenging (Shah et al., 2024). This repre sents a clear need for further development to ensure that hybrid ap proaches can be more systematically and effectively applied in process engineering (Schweidtmann et al., 2024). Most published contributions analyzing how the quality each component or the limited amount of information available for model training impact on the overall hybrid model performance are based on synthetic or simulated datasets, with a notable lack of studies addressing real-world processes in a generaliz able framework. These works are also generally limited to specific ar chitectures (Narayanan et al., 2022). Our methodology is designed for cases in which models are developed for offline analysis and optimization purposes, such as for process design, scale-up and simulation. While several studies have explored hybrid modelling architectures and performance metrics, most contributions do not quantify the relative impact of mechanistic accuracy and data avail ability on the predictive reliability of hybrid models. This work specif ically addresses this gap by analysing how these two factors interact and affect model robustness under both in-silico and industrial conditions. Our investigation encompasses both in-silico and real-world case studies. Specifically, an in-silico study conducted on a fed-batch reactor system for baker’s yeast production is used to address key challenges, such as the limited predicative capability of available mechanistic models and the paucity of data, and questions the effectiveness of hybrid modelling under such constraints. An additional in-silico case study concerning urethane synthesis is presented in the Supplementary Ma terial. Finally, a real-world case study concerning ion-exchange chro matography in biopharmaceutical manufacturing aims at providing insights concerning typical challenges in industrial applications. The paper is organized as follows. In Section 2, we describe the general methodology for the analysis and construction of suitable hybrid modelling configurations. In Section 3, we give details about the in-silico and real-world case studies, respectively. Results are presented and critically discussed in Section 4. Final remarks conclude the manuscript.

## 2. Methodology

### 2.1. General methodology

The methodology is structured into a sequence of steps, each of them contributing to the systematic construction and evaluation of suitable hybrid models (Fig. 1). Here, we aim to assess: (i) the impact of different levels of complexity in the mechanistic component, and (ii) the effect of the amount of information available on the overall hybrid model per formance. These aspects are crucial to evaluate the robustness and generalization capabilities of the methodology under various practical scenarios. The methodological steps are outlined in Fig. 1. 2.1.1. Assuming that the model is practically identifiable (Miao et al., 2011) – i.e., all its parameters can be estimated from measured data – the first step consists in calibrating the mechanistic model using a subset of the available experimental data, either generated in-silico or from a real experiment. In this work, parameter estimation is performed via nonlinear least-squares regression, by minimizing the sum of squared errors (SSE), ϕLS, between the measured outputs yexp,i and the model predictions ̂yi across all available experiments and time points (Sor enson, 1980): ϕLS = ∑ Nsp i=1 [( yexp,i − ̂yi )T( yexp,i −̂yi )] , (2) where Nsp is the total number of samples. The remaining experiments are reserved for validation of the model performance, and, if possible, should cover both conditions within the calibration design space and extrapolative cases, provided they are still representative of the operational domain of interest. This validation strategy allows to assess both model accuracy under familiar conditions, but also its generalization capabilities when applied to unseen regions of the experimental domain. 2.1.2. Step #2 – construction of hybrid model While mechanistic models offer a structured and interpretable description of the underlying physical and chemical phenomena, they often suffer from structural inaccuracies due to simplifications, unmodeled dynamics, or missing mechanisms. To account for process- model mismatch, a hybrid modelling structure that integrates a data- driven component into the mechanistic framework can be used. Spe cifically, we propose an architecture where the mechanistic component provides initial predictions of the states and outputs of the system (Sansana et al., 2021); The data-driven component takes the mechanistic component outputs, along with the experimental inputs available, and is constructed to model the estimation error of the mechanistic compo nent, thus compensating for the observed mismatch (Fig. 2). The proposed architecture differs from the typical serial or parallel configurations reported in the literature (Sch¨afer et al., 2020; Shah et al., 2024). It combines elements of both approaches: similar to parallel structures, the data-driven component corrects the output of the mechanistic model by compensating for its systematic deviations. At the same time, it shares a feature with serial structures, since the data-driven model leverages the information provided by the mechanistic model itself. This results in a formulation that benefits from both strategies, without being a conventional instance of either. Random forest (RF) models are employed as data-driven components (Breiman, 2001). They make output predictions by combining the out comes from a sequence of regression decision trees, called forest; each tree depends on the values of a random vector sampled independently and with the same distribution for all trees in the forest. The output value for the RF is simply the average value of the outputs for every decision tree in the forest. The selection of RFs as data-driven component is motivated by their ability to capture nonlinear relationships, and because they tend to perform well in low-data regimes while exhibiting relatively low risk of overfitting (He and Garcia, 2009). However, it should be noted that the signal predicted by RFs may appear noisy, since the implementation does not account for temporal autocorrelation. As a result, the pre dictions may lack smoothness, and a filtering step was applied to dampen these fluctuations (Blackledget, 2006). The moving average ̃yi of a series yi over a window size ω is defined as: ̃yi = 1 ω ∑ k j=−k yi+j, (3) where ω = 2k +1 is the window size, and k is the number of data points on either side of yi. The window size ω should be large enough to smooth high-frequency noise, while at the same time retain the significant fea tures of the signal, i.e., the predicted output. The key hyperparameter of a RF is the number of trees in the forest; in this work, the optimal value is determined by minimizing the root- mean squared error through cross-validation (He and Garcia, 2009).

In the construction of the hybrid model configuration, the data- driven component is the only one subject to parameter estimation (i. e., the decision rules at each tree node). The mechanistic model pa rameters, which have been calibrated beforehand (see Section 2.1.1), are kept fixed. This defines our sequential approach, where the mech anistic component is first calibrated independently, and subsequently used as the static backbone of the hybrid configuration. While this sequential approach may not guarantee global optimality in terms of overall model performance (Schweidtmann et al., 2024), it is deliber ately adopted here for two reasons: (i) as the mechanistic component is assumed to represent the best available knowledge, it serves as fixed backbone of the hybrid model structure, and (ii) avoiding re-calibration of the mechanistic part allows for a clean and interpretable comparison across different scenarios, isolating the contribution and compensatory effect of the data-driven component without the influence of re-estimating the mechanistic parameters. A simultaneous approach may lead to global optimality in terms of prediction accuracy. However, such an approach was not considered since it also introduces additional complexity: (i) a risk of losing parameter interpretability, as the optimization may compensate mech anistic inaccuracies through parameter values with no physical mean ing; (ii) higher computational cost due to the enlarged parameter space. 2.1.3. To this end, the hybrid model performance should be compared against that of the standalone mechanistic and empirical models. ### 3.2. Ion-exchange chromatography

#### 3.2.1. Process

This case study comes from an industrial biopharmaceutical process. The experimental data concerns monoclonal antibody (mAb) load mate rial with a concentration of 9.12 g L−1, and an average content of heavy and light ends equal to 2.3 % and 1.1 %, respectively. #### 4.2.2. Construction of the hybrid model

We then proceed with the construction of hybrid models following the methodology described in Section 2.1. Two hybrid configurations are developed, each built upon a different mechanistic component, i.e., one based on the LKM coupled with the Langmuir adsorption isotherm, and the other on the LKM coupled with the ASMA isotherm. For both configurations, RF regressors are constructed on four of the five avail able experimental datasets, with a leave-one-out cross-validation scheme to ensure robust model assessment. The inputs to the RF consist of the outlet concentration and the internal states predicted by the mechanistic component (i.e., cmAb and qmAb), together with the con ductivity profile κ. RF hyperparameters are optimised via five-fold cross- validation, and a forest size of 100 trees is selected in both cases. For sake of conciseness, only cross-validation results are shown in Figs. 7 and 8, which illustrate the predictive performance of the hybrid models across the five experimental datasets built on the LKM coupled with Langmuir adsorption isotherm and the ASMA isotherm, respec tively. It is important to state that both hybrid and purely data-driven (Section 4.2.1) configurations exhibit excellent performance in cali bration, consistently achieving low error values across all datasets. Metrics used for the assessment of the predictive capabilities of the constructed models both in calibration and validation (i.e., MAE and PE values) are reported in Table 10. The hybrid model built on the Langmuir isotherm performs reasonably well only for datasets B and D, where system behaviour appears simpler and closer to the assumptions of the Langmuir model. Instead, it fails to correct the poor predictions of the mechanistic model for datasets A, C, and E, where peak broadening and delayed elution dominate the chromatographic response, as confirmed by high PE values. In contrast, the hybrid model based on the ASMA isotherm achieves significantly better accuracy across most scenarios, with notable im provements in datasets A, C, and D. Key outcomes from this analysis can be summarised as follows: • The hybrid model based on the ASMA isotherm shows the best overall predictive performance across the majority of validation cases, with up to 77 % reduction in MAE compared to both the purely data-driven and the mechanistic models (dataset A). ### 4.3. Discussion

The results confirm the potential of hybrid modelling strategies to enhance the predictive performance of standalone mechanistic and data- driven models, based on the level of mechanistic knowledge and the amount of information available for model training. However, the benefit of hybridisation is not unconditional and depends on several interacting factors. Regarding the impact of the two factors analyzed on hybrid model performance, namely (i) mechanistic model accuracy and (ii) amount of information available for model calibration, the following observations can be drawn: (i) Hybrid modelling is not always advantageous. The benefits depend on the specific characteristics of the system under study and the available information. The accuracy of the mechanistic compo nent plays a pivotal role in shaping the hybrid performance: when it provides a good approximation of the process (as observed for mechanistic model #1, the ASMA-based chromatography model, and the urethane case study) hybrid structure significantly improves prediction accuracy and generalisation, even under extrapolative conditions. However, when the mechanistic component is too simplistic or misleading (as for mechanistic model #3 or Langmuir- based chromatography model under salt-varying conditions), and
