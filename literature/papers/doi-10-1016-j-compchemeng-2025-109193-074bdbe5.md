## ABSTRACT

Precipitation is a valuable alternative to recovery processes like chromatography. While the process is a plausible alternative, without experimentation, there is a lack of sufficient understanding of how process operating conditions lead to expected chord length distributions (also referred to as particle size distributions). In this work, a modeling framework is proposed through which, first, the system is specified, then the population balance model (PBM) is constructed. Data is then gathered from experiments and subsequently processed. Afterwards, an optimization problem is defined to regress the PBM parameters to the experimental data. Finally, a surrogate model correlating the PBM parameters with process operating conditions is developed. Thereby, avoiding future experiments and regression of the parameters. The application of the modeling framework is demonstrated using 15 batch experiments for lysozyme precipitation and particle size distribution measurements using focused beam reflectance measurement (FBRM). The current approach helps identify the correlations between process operating conditions and process model (PBM) parameters such as maximum collision efficiency and breakage rate coefficient, which in turn helps in the estimation of the population distribution in the system. The work also proposes the limitations with modeling and data acquisition that pave the way for future research.

## 1. Introduction

Recovery of a biopharmaceutical molecule, such as a protein, usually includes a capture chromatography step, and two chromatographic polishing steps, followed by ultrafiltration/diafiltration. Downstream processing is especially significant as manufacturing costs typically account for about 20% of total product sales, with downstream processing alone representing about 75% of total manufacturing costs (Decker et al., 2023). To overcome the limitations associated with chromatography (see Fig. 1), such as high costs of resins, diffusion issues, and capacity constraints, there is a growing interest in exploring alternative approaches known as ‘‘Anything but Chromatography’’ methodologies (Roque et al., 2020). Currently, nonchromatographic techniques for downstream processing are experiencing renewed interest, including precipitation methods (dos Santos et al., 2017). Precipitation can be utilized as a protein recovery method that is economical, sustainable, and amenable to continuous processing (dos Santos et al., 2017; Walla et al., 2023), and can produce high yields of a pure, functional protein. An early demonstration of this principle is the Cohn precipitation process which is still in use for the separation of blood products (Cohn et al., 1946). There are two separation strategies available for recovering biologically produced molecules such as proteins, by precipitation. The first strategy involves precipitating side components, such as deoxyribonucleic acid (DNA) and host cell proteins (HCP), while keeping the target protein in solution to avoid subsequent protein dissolution and degradation issues. The second strategy involves using precipitation to isolate the target protein itself, which is more convenient because optimal parameters can be selected for one specific target component. Both strategies involve adding a precipitating agent to the proteincontaining solution. The first strategy causes the impurities such as proteases (enzymes that break down proteins) to aggregate and become insoluble, whereas the second strategy causes the protein of interest to precipitate, while other components remain in the soluble phase. The resulting protein aggregates can be easily separated from the solution by centrifugation or filtration. The first strategy is a purification method while the second strategy is a recovery method. Precipitation does not require a complex equipment setup compared to chromatography, a continuous stirred tank (CST) or a tubular vessel with a filter for solid liquid separation works well. It can deal with high process volumes and rising titers and can have shorter process times than chromatography. Furthermore, for applications on a large scale, the volume of the CST is easily scalable, and continuous operation is feasible (Hammerschmidt et al., 2016; Burgstaller et al., 2019). Selective precipitation steps can help skipping two of the usual three chromatography steps, and it is shown that the precipitation offers cost reductions at all stages of the life cycle of a therapeutic antibody (Hammerschmidt et al., 2014). Still challenges to implementation of alternative separation methods in biopharmaceutical manufacturing remain apparent. However, recent advances in upstream process intensification and adoption of new technologies enabling continuous manufacturing have successfully increased the titers of even complex molecules like monoclonal antibodies making these alternative methods potentially more attractive (Müller et al., 2022). There is also a lack of general developmental knowledge for precipitation processes, and concerns over regulatory approval due to limited process data generated by the precipitation process. In this work, we demonstrate a use-case towards recovery of a selected protein by precipitation, using key factors that affect its precipitation from the protein solution. Several factors affect protein precipitation, including pH of the solution, salt concentration, temperature, agitation, protein concentration, presence of co-solvents, and buffer solutions. Variation in pH impacts protein solubility and precipitation by altering the charge distribution on the protein surface. Salt concentration and salt type, as outlined in the Hofmeister series, significantly influences protein stability and solubility due to ions kosmotropic or chaotropic properties. Temperature affects the balance between hydrophobic interactions and other forces that maintain protein solubility. Agitation improves precipitation efficiency by promoting the mixing of precipitants into protein solutions and increasing the probability of collision (Sommer et al., 2015). Protein concentration determines the solubility limit and the likelihood of precipitation (Burgess, 2009). The presence of cosolvents and buffering agents also affects protein solubility and impacts interactions crucial for protein precipitation (dos Santos et al., 2017). These factors such as salt and protein concentration, agitation, etc., collectively shape the outcome of protein precipitation processes by modifying solubility, stability, and protein–solution interactions and a unique set of values of these factors will affect proteins in a different way.

### 1.1. Batch processing and monitoring solutions

Despite the inherent simplicity of batch processing, no established standard for protein precipitation conditions currently exists, primarily because the unique and complex structures of individual proteins lead to distinct biological characteristics. These processes are highly sensitive to various inputs, such as shear forces, salt concentration, and protein concentration. Altering any of these parameters can significantly impact the success of protein recovery by precipitation. Therefore, precise control of process operating conditions is crucial for the successful execution of these processes. Focused Beam Reflectance Measurements (FBRM) and in situ microscopy via Process Video and Measurement (PVM) microscopes are available tools for monitoring precipitation and crystallization operations (Zhang et al., 2024; Walla et al., 2023). In situ monitoring tools are increasingly preferred because of their ability to provide near real-time information without the delays and additional labor associated with ex situ particle analysis techniques. Additionally, in situ methods help mitigate potential artifacts caused by sample preparation, such as changes in precipitate conformation due to dilution or alterations in ionic strength that is often necessary for at-line or off-line sampling. Modern advances allow for the analysis of larger particle populations using near real-time particle imaging techniques. These methods typically involve capturing images with a confocal microscope Computers and Chemical Engineering 200 (2025) 109193 2 A. Ehtesham et al. and highspeed camera, followed by processing with segmentation algorithms to identify and extract various particle properties for each particle (Patchigolla and Wilkinson, 2009). Although techniques such as laser diffraction or electrical sensing zones may not be suitable to accurately determine particle size and shape distribution, microscopy coupled with image analysis, whether ex situ or in situ, is widely used (De Anda et al., 2005). FBRM provides the chord length distribution (CLD) of a particle population, operates by analyzing back-scattering from a rotating monochromatic laser beam. Its ability to function even in concentrated slurries eliminates the need for additional dilution steps or external sampling, making it suitable for online monitoring. However, the effectiveness of FBRM is highly dependent on the optical properties of the surfaces of the particles. It provides highly sensitive real-time tracking of particle size, number, and shape changes without the need for offline sampling. It also detects key process events such as nucleation, growth, and dissolution, for fingerprinting particle system distributions. However, its ability to directly track crystal size is limited to spherical crystals, as the measured CLD corresponds directly to the crystal size distribution (CSD) only in these cases. For non-spherical crystals, mathematical corrections are required to convert CLD data into CSD, which remains a significant challenge. Despite ongoing research to refine these conversion algorithms, this limitation remains a drawback of FBRM technology (Zhang et al., 2024). Although FBRM is well established for monitoring small molecule crystallization, its application to biological macromolecules like proteins remains limited, with only a few publications exploring its potential in this area (Walla et al., 2023). Combining PVM with FBRM enables the differentiation of precipitate identity (De Anda et al., 2005). For example, PVM can be used to differentiate bubbles, oils, and protein crystals that will contribute to the CLD regardless of their identity. PVM also provides turbidity information that can pair well with chord length distribution to fully describe the stages of a precipitation process (De Anda et al., 2005).

### 1.2. Modeling approaches

Modeling plays a crucial role in understanding the fundamentals of a system (Pistikopoulos et al., 2021; Mansouri et al., 2025). While empirical data helps detail observations from the phenomena, models are used to approximate and predict system behaviors that are difficult to measure. Validated models, by simulating processes, help reduce costs by minimizing the need for extensive and time-consuming physical experimentation. Utilizing models can enhance quality control by helping to understand the relationship between process variables and product quality, enabling better control over manufacturing processes. Certain fields, such as chemical engineering, rely on fundamental principles like conservation laws, contrasting with the widespread use of data-driven or black-box modeling in areas like image recognition and natural language processing (Chakraborty et al., 2022). A purely blackbox approach may struggle to generalize to unseen data and risks overfitting, while a strictly mechanistic model faces challenges due to the many variables and parameters that are difficult to measure or estimate (Chakraborty et al., 2022). Therefore, there is an interest in research to develop data-driven models and combining them with first principles/mechanistic knowledge. The combination of mechanistic models and machine learning (ML) models is pivotal in protein precipitation, merging mechanistic models for the phenomenology, and data-driven methods to enhance prediction accuracy and parameter estimation. This integration is crucial in process and product engineering, providing adaptable, reusable, and predictive frameworks for addressing complex challenges (Mansouri et al., 2025). In pharmaceutical production, where process optimization and shortened development cycles are paramount, this hybrid modeling approach offers numerous advantages. The ability of this approach to assimilate various knowledge sources, including prior information and data-driven insights, makes it adaptable to diverse processes and complexities. Leveraging these models combining first-principles/mechanistic knowledge with data from experiments in protein precipitation facilitates parameter optimization and enables the prediction of particle size distribution and agglomeration dynamics during precipitation. This results in increased process efficiency, reduced costs, and improved product quality by tackling operational challenges, optimizing parameters, and making informed decisions based on system understanding and data-driven insights. For more information around this topic, we guide the readers to the following papers, Schubert et al. (1994) and Zhao et al. (2023) for a detailed explanation of using hybrid modeling, particularly in yeast fermentation processes. We would also like to draw attention to Hamilton et al. (2017) that developed hybrid models for predictions of a class of dynamical systems, and Nielsen et al. (2020a), Nielsen et al. (2020b) that used a hybrid model predictive control strategy for particle processes such as crystallization, flocculation or precipitation. Combination of mechanistic models and ML models have been utilized in flocculation and related processes to predict kinetics and process parameters (Nielsen et al., 2020a; Nazemzadeh et al., 2021), assist in optimization, and aid in the development of soft sensors and process control (Nielsen et al., 2020a; Boskabadi et al., 2025, 2024). Several structural approaches exist in the literature for combining mechanistic models and ML models, either in parallel or in series, to improve process modeling. In this work we have adapted a systems approach to model protein precipitation using lysozyme as a model product. The approach is capable of being applied to similar protein systems, both in the population balance modeling (PBM) (to estimate the particle size distribution from the PBM parameters) and in the surrogate modeling/ML methods (to correlate the PBM parameters to process operating conditions) to provide a more comprehensive understanding of the precipitation dynamics of batch proteins (Nielsen et al., 2020a). Furthermore, Samad et al. (2011) developed a generic multi-dimensional model for batch cooling crystallization, and Meisler et al. (2014) modeled crystallization kinetics with a generic modeling framework. The manuscript is organized as follows: Section 2 presents the modeling framework with details on the population balance model and empirical correlations that estimate the particle-size distributions from operating parameters. The details of the surrogate model (using ML) to correlate the PBM parameters against the process operating conditions for fast estimation of PBM parameters, are also mentioned in this section. Section 3 provides details of the experimental procedure and data acquired from the process, using the FBRM probe as well as the experimental setup. Section 4 presents results from the application of the modeling framework to the case of precipitation of lysozyme (as described in Section 3). It also shows how the framework is used for the estimation and prediction for the particle-size distribution, sensitivity with changing PBM parameters, and predictions of the PBM parameters from the process operating conditions via a surrogate model. We also showcase in this investigation the limitations to further future research.

## 2. Modeling framework

The goal of this work is to obtain a dynamic model to predict precipitation of a specific product at different process operating conditions. With limitations on the exact know-how to aid in this estimation of the process dynamics, we have envisioned a framework that combines the population balance model (PBM) for protein precipitation, with a surrogate model for estimation of critical parameters in the PBM. Towards this goal, first, the PBM parameters are regressed based on each experimental dataset of particle size distribution, and subsequently, the surrogate model is trained to predict the regressed PBM parameters as a function of the process operating conditions. The surrogate model can then be used to predict the PBM parameters directly from the operating conditions, which can then be used to predict the particle size distribution (PSD) using the PBM model. ### 2.1. Step 1: System specification

To design an efficient protein precipitation process, it is essential to understand the system and develop predictive models that can save both time and costs. We first chose a known and robust protein precipitation system to generate data for modeling. Then, a mechanistic model is employed to provide accurate predictions of the system’s behavior. In this work, a one-dimensional PBM is used as the mechanistic model to predict the dynamics of the precipitation process. Different phenomena and mechanisms can lead to the birth and death of particles. These phenomena include agglomeration, growth, nucleation, shrinkage and breakage, etc. The PBM contains parameters that will be discussed later and are either difficult to measure or estimate. ML methods can be used to assist in estimating these parameters. It is assumed in this study that only agglomeration and breakage phenomena are present, the effects of nucleation and growth are neglected. At the start of the experiment, all particles are assumed to be primary particles, as lysozyme crystals are initially seeded into the solution, thus the assumption of no nucleation. Multiple coefficients are employed within the equations to characterize the interrelations within the system. The PBM considered in this study is one-dimensional, describing the evolution of the mean chord length of protein precipitates as measured by FBRM. The model includes agglomeration and breakage phenomena in a single dimension, as this is sufficient to capture the primary dynamics of the system without introducing unnecessary complexity. A one-dimensional PBM is justified because the objective is to model the dynamic changes in chord length distribution rather than tracking the full particle size distribution. While a higher-dimensional PBM could provide more detailed insights, the available measurement technique (FBRM) primarily provides chord length distributions, making a 1D representation the most practical choice.

### 2.4. Step 4: Data preprocessing

The data acquired is converted to a chord-length distribution based on the discretized scheme, this means that the measurements are put into their respective particle size bins made in the discretization step to ensure the same resolution with the modeling results. These distributions are then normalized to preserve the dynamic characteristics of the system and ensure consistency across different experiments and simulation runs. The normalization step is crucial, as it aligns the scale of experimental observations with that of the model, particularly in terms of particle population, making the comparison between the two meaningful and accurate. Additionally, this transformation enables interpolation across chord-length ranges that may not have been directly captured during the measurements, allowing for a more continuous and representative distribution to be used in model evaluation. To ensure numerical stability and robustness during the simulation and optimization, further preprocessing is applied. Negative values in the simulated outputs are removed to maintain physical realism, and a minimum threshold is imposed to prevent division by zero during subsequent calculations. ### 2.6. Step 6: Surrogate development and application

A key problem to be addressed by the framework is to correlate the PBM model parameters, specifically 𝛼max and 𝐸, with the process operating conditions. To correlate the process operating conditions to the parameters of the PBM, a surrogate model is required that can adequately estimate these PBM parameters. In our case, an artificial neural network-structure with 1 inputlayer with 4 inputs (corresponding to the process operating conditions), 1-output layer with 2 outputs (corresponding to 𝛼max and 𝐸), and 2 hidden layers with 64 neurons and 32 neurons, respectively, was used. The model has in total 3 layers apart from the input layer, with a ReLU activation function after each layer. Implementing dropout layers helps the model by reducing over-fitting. The neural network architecture was built using the PyTorch module on Python. Supplementary material (Table S4) provides more details about the neural network structure. Details on training are elucidated in Step 6: Surrogate development and application. The surrogate model is structured to take the process operating conditions, such as protein concentration, salt concentration, rotation speed, and temperature for each experiment as inputs, to predict the PBM parameters (𝛼max and 𝐸) for each experiment. Given the computational cost to predict the PBM parameters for each individual experiment, and the experimental effort to collect the process operating conditions data alongside the PSD, a surrogate model is required to correlate the process operating conditions to PBM parameters, for better design of experiments. This trained model can be used to predict the optimized 𝛼max and 𝐸, obtained after step 5. To correlate the process operating conditions to the optimized parameters of the PBM, any ML model may be used. Mean Squared Error (MSE) loss function and Adam optimizer are used to train the surrogate model that correlates the process operating conditions to PBM parameters. The MSE loss calculates the average of the squares of the differences between the predicted and actual PBM parameter values (which are obtained post-optimization in step 5), inherently penalizing larger errors more significantly; this quality makes it especially suited for regression tasks where precise numerical predictions are crucial. Since the number of samples is minimal, it was decided to use crossvalidation and training to improve the regularization of the model. The model was trained on 10,000 epochs to ensure sufficient learning. The neural network’s loss optimizer used mean squared error between the predictions and the training values as the objective function. More information is given in the supplementary material (Section S6). At the end of step 6, we have successfully obtained PBM parameters, i.e., the regressed PBM parameters for each experiment. We have a surrogate model that can predict the regressed process parameters from the process operating conditions.

## 3. Lysozyme precipitation: Experimental setup

We chose a simple and robust protein–buffer system of lysozyme in sodium acetate pH 4.0 to demonstrate the usefulness of process analytical technologies. At low pH, lysozyme is highly charged and will not interact with itself in solution. The addition of salts like sodium chloride influences precipitation by modifying interactions, with the rate depending on solution molarity. This well-characterized crystallization system of lysozyme in sodium acetate pH 4.1 buffer was chosen as a starting point for developing a robust precipitation system which preserves functional proteins (Judge et al., 1999). Lysozyme has a pI value of 10.7, and is therefore expected to be fully charged at pH 4.1 (Baron et al., 2016). The published lysozyme sodium acetate with NaCl process produced tetragonal crystals without shear, suggesting that the interaction kinetics were amenable to a controllable precipitation process. Shear was added to test precipitation kinetics at various protein, salt, temperature, and agitation rate combinations. The 0.1 M sodium acetate pH 4.1 solution was made in deionized water and adjusted to pH 4.1 with glacial acetic acid. The reported buffering capacity of sodium acetate is pH 3.6 to 5.6. Lysozyme and NaCl were combined in the base sodium acetate buffer. All solutions were filtered through a 0.1 μm filter prior to vessel addition. Precipitation screening was achieved by adding increasing amounts of salt and protein and reacting overnight at 25 ◦C at a moderate shaking speed in a 96-well format (cat. 3788, Corning) and using binary well scoring for visible precipitation.

The differential evolution method, although computationally intensive, running for nearly 7 h (2.2 GHz Intel(R) Core(TM) i9-13900HX), proved more effective. The optimization parameters were selected to balance computational efficiency with the need for a thorough search, employing a high population size (popsize) and 3 iterations. To balance it out, a broader range for mutation and a higher value for recombination was defined to allow the optimizer to diversify. The seed number is used to ensure the reproducibility of the results. The optimizer’s internal variables are given in detail in the supplementary material (Table S3).

The jumps observed in the median predictions could be explained by the properties of the median as a measure of the center in the data. Unlike the mean, which continuously reflects small changes in all data points, the median is determined solely by the middle value of an ordered dataset. Consequently, minor fluctuations in the underlying distribution may not alter the median until a sufficient number of data points shift, leading to fast changes rather than smooth transitions. This characteristic means that even if the particles grow in size gradually, the median may only update once the central portion of the data crosses a threshold, thereby producing noticeable jumps in the prediction trend. Increasing the breakage rate coefficient to a number above 3 will lead to mathematical instability in the model and it might be because of assumptions in the modified breakage distribution function which will lead to the creation of more particles than what is should be and it results in very high agglomeration at the end of the experiment. This is important to understand the range of values within which the PBM parameters can change, or useful for any optimizer that needs boundaries for the inputs.

#### 4.4.2. Initial condition and system information

To balance computational efficiency with accuracy, the total number of particle bins (𝑚) was set to 30. This binning choice ensures a sufficient resolution to capture particle distribution dynamics effectively. Any number above 30 will lead to significantly higher computational time. As can be seen in Fig. 9, although increasing the number of bins improves accuracy, using around 30 bins strikes a balance by being sufficiently close to larger values while significantly reducing computational time. The initial condition was constructed by assuming that all particles present, based on the specific protein concentration and reaction volume, act as primary particles in the experiment. Further details are provided in the supplementary material (Table S2). The ODE system was then solved using the solve_ivp function from Python’s SciPy library, with the LSODA method chosen due to its robust handling of stiffness, which enhances solution stability and convergence. Further details about the solver are provided in the supplementary material (section 2.2).

## 5. Conclusion

In this work, we have explored dynamical modeling and ML to elucidate the mechanisms of protein precipitation from process operating conditions. The PBM model allows us to create a dependence between its critical parameters to the measured PSD. However, due to the computational and experimental effort to obtain these parameters, we have presented a surrogate modeling strategy to predict the PBM parameters from the process operating conditions data. We have also shown the use of measured distributions from the FBRM probe for the amount of particles in a length scale to get regressed PBM parameters. In this study, the modeling approach has been presented to address the recovery of proteins like lysosymes. PBMs are utilized to predict PSDs, and process operating conditions are correlated with PBM parameters, in this work. The combination of PBM with a surrogate model (ANN) helps find the parameters of the mechanistic dynamical model. While in this work KL divergence was used to regress the PBM parameters to PSD, other approaches could involve using other objective functions like energy distance and maximum mean discrepancy, to relate FBRM probe measurements to the number of particles rather than just the distribution. Future work could also consider additional process operating conditions, tuning additional PBM parameters for better accuracy. A global sensitivity analysis could help identify the most influential parameters while addressing measurement errors, and prediction uncertainties would improve the model’s reliability. While the approach is generalizable for any protein with similar properties to lysozyme, we observe that the main changes to the model will relate to fine-tuning the correlations and the ML model. We note that the amount of data required for a generalizable model was not realized for the ML model, as some spurious correlations were extrapolated by the model. To alleviate this, we showed how creating different folds of cross-validated data can be used to train a surrogate model for the correlation between the process operating conditions and the PBM parameters. Further, the optimized values of parameters for collision efficiency and breakage rate coefficient suffer from suboptimal training. Despite this limitation, certain correlations obtained through the surrogate approach adhere to a mechanistically plausible interpretation, lending credibility to the model and the modeling framework. These limitations with the modeling can be subverted with accelerated computing and global optimization strategies that require more information on the PBM parameters.
