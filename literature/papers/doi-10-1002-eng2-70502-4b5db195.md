## ABSTRACT

Recent hardware developments within industrial digitalization make more evident the need of high-fidelity models to exploit process system engineering potential in biomanufacturing. Hybrid models, which combine first-principles (white-box) and data-driven (black-box) approaches, aim to leverage the strengths of both methodologies to mitigate their individual limitations to provide high predictive power. This article explores the integration of hybrid modeling techniques in bioprocess engineering, emphasizing their potential to facilitate product development, accelerate experimental design, enhance process monitoring, drive optimization and catalyze the implementation of digitalization strategies to achieve a more robust and sustainable production in the framework of the goals set by the UN 2030 agenda. In this review, we analyze 270 publications from the early 1990s to 2024, extracting application domains, hybrid-model architectures, implementation software, and nonparametric output structures. Our findings highlight marked improvements in predictive accuracy, and extrapolation capabilities while revealing persistent challenges in integrating mechanistic and empirical components. This review on hybrid modeling implementations shows their potential to enhance accuracy, calibration, and extrapolation capabilities, highly required for bioprocesses. This work underscores the importance of integrating mechanistic and data-driven approaches to improve the flexibility and interpretability of models in complex engineering systems, offering significant implications for more sustainable and innovative bioprocesses. 1 | Introduction Sustainability has become a critical focus in addressing global challenges, as it ensures that current development meets the needs of present generations without compromising the ability of future generations to meet their own needs. Second and third generation biorefineries (2G and 3G) allow biomass and agricultural wastes to take full potential, without compromising food security, which in turn makes them very attractive for adding value to waste [2]. Bioprocesses offer a promising avenue to meet sustainability goals by reducing reliance on fossil fuels and lowering carbon footprints [3]. However, they also pose significant challenges, including the complexity of biological systems and the need to balance economic viability with ecological responsibility [4]. Moreover, biorefineries are often complex processes that involve many steps, posing a technical challenge from their inception through the implementation. One of the main challenges of biorefineries takes place in the bioreaction stage. Biotransformations are intricate, and their mechanisms are usually not well understood, which leads to low titer, productivity, and yield. Process systems engineering (PSE) plays a vital role in addressing these challenges by integrating knowledge from engineering, mathematics, and computer science to optimize bioprocesses [5]. PSE methods enable the design, control, and operation of bioprocess systems in a way that improves efficiency and minimizes environmental impact. For instance, through advanced modeling, simulation, and optimization techniques, PSE can help predict the behavior of biological systems, optimize resource usage, and identify bottlenecks in production [6, 7]. In PSE, modeling takes a central role for process simulation and its use [8, 9]. Models can administrate information from two main perspectives: structural (our understanding of the underlying mechanisms) and the model parametrization (the nature of the numeric techniques used to describe the relationships between data). From the structural point of view, first-principles, mechanistic or phenomenological models represent a wide class of models, usually called white-box, due to being developed primarily on conservation equations and physical laws. First principles models are constructed through deductive reasoning, wherein the model equations are derived from existing knowledge of the system, using established relationships such as conservation laws. Mechanistic models usually comprise a system of differential algebraic equations (DAEs). An important characteristic of mechanistic models is that they can be extrapolated to process conditions outside of the calibration space, thanks in part to being developed from conservation laws, although this is not always the case [10]. In contrast, data-driven models typically employ an inductive approach, wherein a generic mathematical model is fitted to map inputs to outputs, without considering the intrinsic structure of the phenomena, unlike their white-box counterparts [10–12]. Data-driven techniques usually take the form of arbitrary functions to model the relations between inputs and outputs. Gray models usually fall in between, combining mechanistic knowledge and data-driven techniques [13, 14]. From another perspective, models can be classified regarding the form of model parametrization. Parametric models are established beforehand based on the knowledge of the process [15]. These models possess a finite number of parameters, often characterized by physical significance, which vary according to the model’s complexity [11]. In contrast, nonparametric models are solely derived from data, lacking predefined parameters [15]. The term “nonparametric” does not mean that the model is built without parameters, but that the number of them is variable, not fixed in advance by knowledge, and that they commonly lack physical significance [11]. In nonparametric modeling, mathematical techniques such as algebraic, series expansions, machine learning (ML), and other black-box approaches are used to derive insights from inputs to outputs. These techniques are also called “surrogate models” when they are used to approximate the behavior of more complex and computationally costly models [16] and are used to describe the relevant process behavior exclusively from model-generated data, to perform calculations faster such as optimization [17, 18]. Typically, they have great predicting power inside the space of their calibration, but extrapolation is not advisable [19]. Developing parametric models requires extensive domain expertise, making the process time-intensive and complex, often involving experts from multiple disciplines. Nonparametric models offer greater flexibility as they do not require prior mechanistic knowledge, making them particularly useful when the underlying system is poorly understood or difficult to describe mathematically. These models can efficiently capture complex, nonlinear relationships within data, often uncovering hidden patterns that parametric models might miss. However, their flexibility comes at a cost. Nonparametric models typically require large amounts of high-quality data for training, and their computational cost is significantly higher, particularly during the training phase. Additionally, they are prone to overfitting and overparameterization, making them less robust, and they often struggle with extrapolation beyond the observed data range. They are usually not reliable enough to extrapolate to conditions outside of their calibration space; however, they have great interpolation capabilities within it. Another critical drawback is their lack of interpretability, as they do not rely on physical constraints or conservation laws, which can make it difficult to understand the underlying mechanisms driving predictions. From previous experience, there is no ideal model, with both parametric and nonparametric models having strengths and shortcomings that not only depend on system understanding, but also on data and computational power. As a potential trade-off, the scientific community has proposed hybrid modeling, also called hybrid semi-parametric modeling, to overcome the challenges that arise from parametric and nonparametric modeling, while retaining most of their advantages. Notice that researchers do not always agree on the definition and use of the term “hybrid model.” Some authors claim to have developed hybrid models, when they combine different mechanistic and empiric knowledge (mathematical relationships that provide reliable approximations but are not built using mechanistic knowledge) such as correlations for heat and mass transfer coefficients, for example, [28–34]. Besides, the term has been used when modified structures apply to pure nonparametric approaches for predictions. These modifications usually entail combinations of artificial neural networks (ANNs) with other ML techniques or optimization algorithms [35–40]. Finally, “hybrid modeling” has been applied to other approaches such as Computational Fluid Dynamics applications that integrate various numerical methods [41] and process system engineering simulations that use multiple software tools to perform the simulations [42–44]. Here in, “hybrid modeling” represents a modeling paradigm that seeks to combine the strengths of both parametric and nonparametric modeling while minimizing their individual disadvantages. Hybrid modeling is an emerging approach gaining traction; thus there are recent reviews in the literature detailing the applications of hybrid modeling to separation processes [45], biochemical processes using neural networks [24, 46, 47], wastewater treatment (WTT) [48]; chemical, petroleum and energy systems [46, 49]. For bioprocesses specifically, recent reviews include topics such as the role of hybrid models in bioprocess scale-up [50]; the applications of hybrid modeling to systems biology for mammalian cell culture development [51], and the prospects of hybrid modeling for industry 4.0 and 5.0 [20, 23]. Those contributions delve into the details of relevant discussions. However, they lack quantitative and qualitative trends and information on how hybrid models have been implemented in bioprocesses. This study provides the main characteristics of hybrid models, such as structure, knowledge integration, performance assessment, data requirements, and challenges. This contribution focuses on the hybrid modeling of reactions in bioprocesses, aggregating information from 270 contributions screened down from 5890 identified from a bibliometric search. From there, the target is to identify trends such as the types of bioprocesses to which hybrid modeling has been applied, the preferred architectures/structures/design patterns, the most used nonparametric techniques, the scale of the experimental data used to develop the models, the mode of operation of the experiments, and software used for building the models, as well as cross-correlations between these categories. Figure 1 shows the knowledge basis and mathematical representations for parametric, nonparametric, and hybrid modeling. Parametric models heavily rely on first principles and prior knowledge, using conservation laws, mass-energy-momentum balances, kinetic mechanisms, thermodynamic relationships, and transport laws to describe the system. Parametric models are usually expressed as algebraic equations, DAEs or partial differential equations (PDEs), with data being mostly used for parameter identification (hence the dotted green line in Figure 1). Nonparametric modeling approaches are usually data-hungry [23, 26, 45, 50] and build their knowledge exclusively from process data, extracting no information from first principles. Nonparametric models are usually expressed as data-driven techniques such as ANNs, Gaussian Processes (GPs), and support vector machines (SVMs) or Autoregressive models (ARX, ARMAX, ARIMA). Hybrid modeling aims to compromise both approaches in the middle, needing fewer data than nonparametric models, but without requiring prior knowledge as extensive as parametric models do. Hybrid models can be expressed in a wide range of structures, mixing the data-driven techniques used for nonparametric modeling and the mechanistic knowledge expressed in parametric models. It is important to note that hybrid models do not necessarily use their parametric and nonparametric components in the same ratio. Thus, the spectrum seen in Figure 1 signals that hybrid models can be “darker” if they rely more on the nonparametric techniques than the mechanistic ones, or “lighter” if the inverse is true. Literature often claims that hybrid modeling leverages most of the advantages of parametric and nonparametric modeling while minimizing its disadvantages. Although this is empirically seen in an increased performance when compared against full mechanistic models, there are sparse mathematical proofs. It has been proven that giving more structure to the nonparametric subcomponent via an additive term helps to reduce the asymptotic variance of the parametric parameters [53]. These design patterns generalize, unify, and formalize diverse modeling approaches across various domains and applications. The following sections introduce these patterns and illustrate them through selected relevant studies from the literature. Notice that the chemical engineering literature has used the term “architecture” [11, 23, 56]. Recently, a distinction between “design pattern” and “architecture” was proposed because any model architecture can have different combinations of different design patterns, working in parallel, in series or nested inside another [26]. In the present work, “architecture” and “design pattern” are synonyms, whereas “structure” is used to discuss the different combinations and arrangements of the design patterns. It is important to note that a model structure can comprise one or several design patterns. In this architecture, the white box is usually composed of conservation laws, mass, heat, or momentum transfer relations that describe the process. The black box typically represents the kinetics of the process or the transport coefficients, for which there is no general valid model [10, 47, 57]. This architecture is very useful when there is no precise knowledge of the underlying mechanisms, but there is enough data to identify the underlying patterns. Another scenario is presented when there is a huge amount of data that can be exploited to model the underlying mechanisms [58, 59]. The “physics-based preprocessing” design pattern is mostly used in data sparse environments, primarily using data augmentation [12]. In this architecture, the white-box model predictions are used to establish a link between the state of the system and certain parameters [11]. Some examples include: the prediction of cell culture using ANNs [60] where authors used an ANN to model some reaction rates in their dynamic balance flux model using other mechanistic reaction rates and states and inputs to the data-driven components, a fed-batch process for producing penicillin [61] taking advantage of Kalman filters to estimate and reconstruct some states from noisy data and predict fault detections, the control of biomass in protein production using Pichia pastoris [62] implementing a soft-sensor to predict biomass concentration based on online measurements of CO2 utilizing a multi-linear regression that takes advantage of the mechanistic carbon balance. When utilizing serial architectures, the training can be done in two different ways: coupling the identification of the parametric and nonparametric components simultaneously or decoupling the identification, usually calibrating a parametric structure and then calibrating a nonparametric component to account for the deficiencies of the parametric component. 3.2.2 | Parallel Architecture The parallel architecture (“delta model” design pattern) applies if there is a process model (white box) that captures the underlying mechanisms but has limited predictive power due to structural factors not accounted for (unmodeled effects, nonlinearities, dynamic behavior) [26]. The predictive power of the model can be enhanced using nonparametric techniques, although their performance remains poor outside of the training region [11, 23]. This structure is interesting if the system can be decoupled into non-interacting parts, that is, static non-linear and dynamic linear behavior. This modeling approach has been applied to cases such as predicting phosphorus removal in a WTT process [63] correcting the model’s mismatch by adding the output of an ANN to the mechanistic reaction rates to enhance prediction capabilities; using Kalman filters to correct bias from mechanistic model predictions and enabling higher forecasting capabilities in each integration time in ethanol fermentations for biofuel production [64]; using ANNs to correct the predictions of parametric models in cell apoptosis [65]; applying corrections to the activated sludge model in an industrial cokes wastewater-plant [66] correcting the activated sludge model by using five different data-driven techniques like ANNs and multiple linear regressions; predicting biomass concentration in a baker’s yeast fermentation [67] utilizing a mixture of experts, where each data-driven component is used to predict the states and a gating function combines them for enhancing prediction capabilities; studying polymerization processes by simplifying the parametric structure of the model [68] or fed-batch penicillin fermentations [15] where simplified models are proposed and the data-driven techniques take full advantage of residuals and state predictions to have similar performance to more computationally demanding models. Researchers have proposed three main ways for approaching the parallel structure, shown in Figure 3 [11]. The (a) configuration is called “superposition” where the black box output is summed or subtracted from the white box model prediction to enhance the model performance. This configuration works as a bias correction, changing the output variance and shape. This is the most used parallel structure, first proposed in bioprocesses to study a fed-batch penicillin fermentation [15]. Configuration (b) is called “multiplication” because the output of the model is the multiplication of the outputs predicted by the black box and white box, working as a correction factor (amplification or mitigation of the parametric outputs). It was first utilized in bioprocesses for a bakers’ yeast production process [17]. The (c) configuration is applied when the outputs of the black box and white box are transformed by either a black box or a white box model [69], as such, weight factors (w1 and w2) are applied to combine them and obtain the final prediction. It is important to note that the inputs to the nonparametric and parametric parts could be different (as evidenced in Figure 3); therefore, the nonparametric component might obtain knowledge from information that the parametric models do not consider in their formulation. 3.2.3 | Physical Constraints Physical constraints represent a hybrid modeling design pattern that integrates domain knowledge—such as conservation laws—into the structure, parameters, or outputs of a data-driven model [20, 26, 70]. The white box should be limited to knowledge that represents the physical interactions of the system [25]. Therefore, mathematical techniques that build insights from data are highly valuable. Non-parametric and data-driven techniques can be broadly classified in the following categories, with accompanying examples of their applications to bioprocesses: • Algebraic expressions such as polynomials or rational functions for predicting the pH and acetic acid production of Kombucha fermentations [83], and sigmoidal and exponential functions for describing methane production during the anaerobic digestion of orange peel waste [84]. • Series expressions such as Fourier series used for optimizing the pH profile for controlling the process of the yeast Wickerhamomyces anomalus against the spoilage yeast Zygosaccharomyces rouxii in wine pre-fermentation [85]. • Statistical models such as principal component analysis (PCA) for identifying the correlations between variables for monoclonal antibodies production [86], and multivariate data analysis (MVDA) for predicting the production of adenovirus using human embryonic kidney cells (HEK 293) [87]. • Time-series autoregressive approaches such as ARMAX for controlling dissolved oxygen in a S. cerevisiae fermentation [88], ARIMA for modeling and predicting a membrane bioreactor (MBR) for treating ampicillin from pharmaceutical wastewater [89] or ARX for predicting biomass concentrations in a lactic acid fermentation [90]. • ML approaches such as ANNs, as well as techniques like GPs or SVMs [91]. An overview of several nonparametric structures such as ANNs, Random Forests (RF), SVMs, GPs, among others has been recently presented [92]. The choice of nonparametric technique coupled with the hybrid model’s structure heavily influences the development and the strategy for training and validation of the model. 3.5 | Hybrid Model Performance Assessment Hybrid models’ performance and extrapolation capabilities are directly related to the structure of the nonparametric portion of the model. This refers not only to whether the architecture is parallel or serial but also to the configuration and the specific model structure of the nonparametric model, particularly its size (e.g., layers of an ANN, number of neurons per layer, type of activation function, etc.) [11]. The size of the data-driven technique can be assessed using the Akaike Information Criteria (AIC) or Bayesian Information Criteria (BIC), the latter being preferred for models with a high number of parameters [55, 57]. For discriminating between models, Friedman’s test can be applied [93]. In general, there should be a trade-off between estimation quality and the number of parameters, to avoid overfitting or underfitting issues, which should also be addressed in training and validation. To compare hybrid models’ performance to traditional parametric and nonparametric models, researchers have also used more conventional metrics such as the coefficient of determination r2 despite not being suited for nonlinear estimators in biochemical systems [94], the mean squared error (MSE) and its derivatives such as root mean square error (RMSE) or normalized MSE (NMSE), error measurements such as mean absolute percentage error (MAPE), mean absolute error (MAE), among others. When extrapolating the hybrid model’s predictions outside the training domain, some assessment of the validity of the extrapolation should be done. Authors have mainly used two complementary criteria: convex-hull criteria and a confidence interval criterion. The convex-hull criteria are used to verify that each empirical model solely interpolates the data gathered during model identification. Meanwhile, the confidence interval criterion evaluates the confidence intervals for the predictions of the hybrid model [95]. Other authors have proposed that the confidence interval of the hybrid model can be estimated using Bayesian optimization, resampling the model’s prediction using Markov Chain Monte Carlo simulations [96]. 3.6 | Hybrid Modeling Requirements for Data Data is essential for discerning the structure and parameters of the hybrid model, and principally all properties of the model (such as prediction quality and extrapolation capabilities) heavily rely both on the quality and quantity of the data [10, 12, 24]. Data is necessary to develop any hybrid model. If there is no previous data, it must be generated, with a systematic exploration of the design space (e.g., using Design of Experiments for a system or a benchmark model). Different techniques can be applied, such as factorial design, sequential design, optimal design, and model-based design methodologies [97]. A valuable alternative is the so-called “Intensified Design of Experiments,” where intra-experimental shifts to key parameters are performed to capture the dynamics of the process [59, 98, 99]. This methodology has been applied specifically to develop hybrid models, yielding great predictive power with a reduced number of experiments in an early stage of model development. ANNs have been widely applied across various bioprocesses, from cell cultivation to pharmaceutical production. Other ML techniques, such as SVMs, have also been utilized, particularly in cell cultivation and WTT. In lesser proportion, parametric techniques such as polynomials and sigmoidal expressions have been applied successfully to metabolic fluxes analysis [136], and lutein production [139]. From an application perspective, various categories of data-driven techniques have been successfully employed in bioprocessing. However, there is no consensus on the most suitable method for each process type, despite a clear preference for ANNs. Over 63% of work targets metabolite production—chiefly fermentation and protein synthesis—while ̃40% address pharmaceutical operations (penicillin, therapeutic proteins, antibodies, CHO cells) [101, 102]. As such, the enhanced prediction capabilities of hybrid models combined with specific approaches to experimental design [59, 98, 99] have led to innovation in the field. WTT comprises ̃10% of cases but leads in industrial uptake, underscoring hybrid models’ aptitude for revealing hidden dynamics under sparse monitoring. In terms of hybrid model architecture, feature learning hybrids dominate despite offering no statistically significant performance advantage over delta or serial patterns. Their popularity likely stems from built-in interpretability, conservation law enforcement, and modular training, which suit the multi-step, partially observed nature of bioprocesses [20]. Ethanol fermentations of S. cerevisiae remain the most intensely studied system—driven by abundant data—while recent work has diversified to include E. coli, P. pastoris, CHO cells (collectively 30%), and a broad “other” category (32%). MATLAB remains the predominant simulation environment, although Python is gaining traction. Model performance assessment still is variant: about a quarter of studies still rely on r2—despite its failure to penalize over parameterization and insensitivity to nonlinear dynamics [94, 254]. On the other hand, 45% of contributions use MSE derivatives to calculate their performance indexes, despite it being highly sensitive to outliers [255]. This last selection might be since it is a default performance index in ML toolboxes. To foster more robust and interpretable assessments, we foresee that these performance indexes could be complemented with information-theoretic and distribution-based measures (e.g., entropy coefficients, Kolmogorov–Smirnov statistics, Log-Cosh and quantile losses) and adopting principled model-comparison criteria such as Watanabe Akaike Information Criterion (WAIC) or Leave-One-Out Cross-Validation Information Criterion (LOO-CVI) [256–259]. Despite three decades of development, hybrid bioprocess modeling still lacks standardized protocols for selecting design patterns, balancing mechanistic and data-driven components, quantifying uncertainty and defining data preprocessing workflows. The field’s reliance on trial and error and experience—evidenced by 374 individual model implementations versus 155 distinct case studies—underscores this gap. Some efforts have been made recently to highlight practical and technical details of implementing hybrid models, such as reviews that present brief guides for developing them [25, 260], but more work is needed. Due to having both a parametric/mechanistic component and a data-driven/nonparametric component, assessing how much uncertainty comes from each remains an unexplored avenue. The field could benefit from recent developments in the ML field, where authors suggest a Monte Carlo approach to generate testing datasets and better capture uncertainty [261]. The absence of standardized guidelines in hybrid bioprocess modeling arises precisely from its defining strength: structural flexibility. Hybrid frameworks can accommodate a wide range of design choices—yet still outperform purely mechanistic models—while demanding far less data than fully nonparametric approaches and preserving a degree of interpretability that renders them practical for industrial deployment. However, this very hybrid character also complicates evaluation: conventional metrics and workflows developed for parametric or nonparametric models often fail to capture the unique tradeoffs in uncertainty quantification and mechanistic parameter estimation inherent to hybrid systems. Numerical workflows in hybrid bioprocess modeling are similarly under specified. Critical steps—data pretreatment methods (both numerical and knowledge based), augmentation strategies, and the rationale for selecting inputs to the data driven module—are frequently omitted, leaving reproducibility and comparability in doubt. Moreover, cross-validation protocols and data partitioning schemes are rarely described, despite their importance for robust training and unbiased assessment of models that rely heavily on high-quality data to capture complex, industrially relevant dynamics. To address these gaps, the community should adopt rigorous information-theoretic tools to quantify intervariable relationships and guide model structure, and standardize transparent cross-validation procedures to ensure reliable, reproducible hybrid model development [262]. Cross validation offers clear advantages—correcting selection bias, quantifying estimation uncertainty (particularly for nonparametric components) and guiding model complexity to prevent overfitting [263]. Yet, despite its value, consensus on hyperparameter tuning remains elusive. While many practitioners adhere to the conventional three-way split (training, validation, test) coupled with grid, random or Bayesian searches, limited data often forces a two-set approach (training and validation only). Without systematic cross validation, hybrid models inherit latent selection bias, further obscuring true predictive uncertainty. Although a handful of specialized diagnostic tools exist for assessing hybrid performance, the community still lacks standardized protocols for data partitioning and hyperparameter optimization—an important frontier for improving both transparency and reliability in hybrid bioprocess modeling. The challenges outlined above often originate in insufficient data quantity and quality, underscoring the necessity of integrating hybrid-model data requirements into the experimental design phase. In this context, Intensified Design of Experiments (iDoE) offers a compelling solution: by strategically varying inputs and conditions, iDoE minimizes the total number of trials while maximizing the informational content of each run, thereby furnishing richer datasets that better support the calibration and validation of hybrid bioprocess models [59, 98, 99, 117]. Of course, this strategy is also limited by monitoring capabilities, since online data acquisition of primary variables at high frequency still is a challenge. Developments in process analytical technologies (PAT) definitely will catalyze the advanced modeling strategies as hybrid models. 4.7 | Hybrid Modeling Challenges Certain challenges for implementing hybrid models have been mentioned in the literature [26, 55, 264]:

## 1. The mechanistic part of the hybrid model constrains the

space of solutions obtainable, which helps avoid pitfalls such as negative concentrations. These constraints impose an inductive bias into the responses, which might negatively affect the hybrid model’s performance if the mechanistic part is not suitable for describing the process.

## 2. There are standard techniques for identifying parameters

in statistical or purely parametric models. For hybrid models, only a handful of parameter identification methods have been reported [57, 95, 104, 265]. The major hurdle is the time needed for parameter identification, due to the extensive calculations that should be performed.

## 3. In addressing the initial challenge, the integration of hybrid

models requires a thorough examination of the foundational assumptions inherent in the mechanistic knowledge to be integrated. This task can be approached via two distinct methodologies: Engineering Reports, 2025 19 of 30 25778196, 2025, 12, Downloaded from https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1002/eng2.70502, Wiley Online Library on [13/05/2026]. See the Terms and Conditions (https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/terms-and-conditions) on Wiley Online Library for rules of use; OA articles are governed by the applicable Creative Commons License a. The incremental approach, where the most basic assumptions are made, and more complex interactions are added until the model performance decreases. b. The removal approach, where the most complex fundamental structure is evaluated first, and then features are removed iteratively to determine what part is hindering the model’s performance. Both approaches are time consuming, so model development will require some experience or an analysis of a risk of mechanistic knowledge being incorrect.

## 4. Validating and verifying a hybrid model is another challenge, because there needs to be a balance of complexity,

accuracy, interpretability and scalability which is hard to optimize. Moreover, in hybrid models, nonparametric and parametric components can introduce distinct sources of uncertainty and error that must be carefully managed [26]. 5. The level of “grayness” in hybrid models—the balance between mechanistic knowledge and data-driven components—can significantly impact performance. As illustrated by Rogers et al. [183], incorporating too much or too little mechanistic knowledge can lead to bias or overfitting, respectively, thus hindering model performance. This underscores the need for meticulous calibration and validation of hybrid models to ensure optimal balance and performance. Regardless of advancements in key areas, still there is not a unified framework for determining the valid region for extrapolation of hybrid models. Besides the challenges addressed before, we propose the following ones that emerge from the literature review:

## 1. There

are no consumer-ready applications built on open-source software for building hybrid models easily, which hinders its adoption by industry, due to the need for specialized personnel to build, operate and maintain the models. Although some custom toolboxes for MATLAB like HYBMOD [125, 220, 222, 233] exist, source code is poorly documented, and a licensing agreement should be signed.

## 2. As with many pure data-driven techniques, there is not

a consolidated framework that allows to select the proper nonparametric technique a priori, meaning that a costly process of trial and error is necessary to build hybrid models.

## 3. Like the previous challenge, there is a lack of a general and

unified framework for deciding which type of hybrid model structure/design pattern is best suited for an application, which also results in a resource-intensive search.

## 4. As with many nonparametric techniques, how data should

be split between training, testing and validations has no consensus. Authors commonly only use training and validation splits due to constraints on the availability of high-quality data, while few report using datasets for the three purposes. 5. There is no consensus on the training hierarchy of the parametric and nonparametric components of hybrid models. When using the “delta model” design pattern, the training of each subcomponent is done separately. However, when using the other design patterns, there are no clear guidelines on how to address the identification of the parametric components and the nonparametric components of hybrid models. Some authors choose the best pure parametric model structure available, start hybridizing and then calibrating the nonparametric structure over it [214]. Other authors do the calibration of both components at the same time [266, 267]. Whether one approach yields better results than the other needs to be further investigated. 4.8 | Hybrid Modeling Perspectives Looking forward, several promising avenues for future research and development in hybrid semi-parametric modeling are clear. One key area is the continued refinement of model integration techniques. Advancements and applications of deep learning [125, 220, 222] and the development of sophisticated neural network architectures offer opportunities to enhance the nonparametric components of hybrid models. The application of Bayesian inference, could improve the accuracy and confidence of predictions by incorporating uncertainty quantification into the modeling process [248]. Additionally, expanding the application of hybrid models to new bioprocesses and products will be crucial. As the bioprocessing industry continues to innovate and develop new products, hybrid models will need to adapt and evolve to meet these emerging challenges. The growing interest from commercial and industrial sectors, including companies like AspenTech and DataHow AG, as well as the pharmaceutical industry, further underscores the relevance and applicability of hybrid models in real-world settings. The development of standardized frameworks and best practices for hybrid model implementation could facilitate broader adoption and integration into industrial processes. Authors should remain open-minded and explore solutions and implementations beyond hybrid modeling in bioprocesses or applied mathematics or computer science; for instance, disciplines like agriculture and meteorology have yielded promising hybrid modeling strategies [268–270]. Although implementations are dependent on data quality and specific to each field, general insights and strategies can be adapted. Hybrid semi-parametric models represent a powerful tool for advancing bioprocess modeling and optimization. Integrating mechanistic and data-driven approaches offers a pathway to more accurate, reliable, and scalable models that can meet the complex demands of modern bioprocessing.
